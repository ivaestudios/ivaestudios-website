Journal · Locations

Photo locations in the Riviera Maya — read by register of light.

From Tulum to Maroma, the corridor is not a single look. It is four registers of light coexisting on one coast: golden-hour warm, open-shade soft, marina blue, editorial contrast. The location decides which one we walk into.

April 2026 · 13 min read · By Vianey Ortega

The Riviera Maya is one hundred and twenty kilometres of coast that reads, on a feed, like four different countries. The mistake is to treat it as a single location. The work is to choose, per session, which register of light the location is built for — and to schedule the day around it.

The four registers, named

Every IVAE session sits inside one of four registers. The location decides the register; the register decides the time of day; the time decides the wardrobe. We hold the order strictly. The four:

Golden hour, warm. The first hour after sunrise and the last hour before sunset on east-facing beach. Skin tones run honey, the sand reads cream, the water keeps a pale aquamarine. Tulum, Akumal, the Maroma stretch — this is their best hour.

Open shade, soft. Architecture-led light. Resort corridors, palm canopies, beachfront palapa interiors at four-thirty in the afternoon. The skin reads even, the colour holds, the wind is reduced. Mayakoba and the Tulum boutique-hotel hour live here.

Marina, Atlantic blue. Cooler, harder light off the water. Mid-morning at Puerto Aventuras, the Playa del Carmen marina, any session that puts boats and concrete into the frame. The register the editorial press calls "Mediterranean."

Editorial, contrast. Mid-day cenotes, archive interiors, high-noon ruins under canopy. Hard shafts of light, deep shadow. Casa Malca's gallery rooms, Cenote Calavera's beam, the underbrush at Coba.

«Every session balances four registers of light. The grid is a system, not a collection.»

Tulum — boutique-resort heart

Tulum is open shade and golden hour, almost never marina. The strongest sessions are interior-first: Hotel Bardo's courtyard, Be Tulum's pool corridor, Casa Malca's gallery rooms. The east-facing beach holds for the first forty-five minutes after sunrise, then goes flat until five. Cenote Calavera and Gran Cenote sit ten minutes inland for the editorial-contrast inserts. We treat Tulum as a one-day chapter inside a Riviera Maya itinerary; the full direction lives in our Tulum guide.

Akumal — golden hour, gentler

Akumal Bay sits twenty-five minutes north of Tulum and offers the calmest water on the corridor — a half-moon of reef-protected coast that holds a clean reflection at sunrise and again at last light. The register is golden-hour warm, but quieter than Tulum: less wind, smaller scale, palms closer to the waterline. We recommend Akumal for families with small children and for anniversary couples who want a private hour without resort traffic. The two beach clubs at the south end coordinate non-peak access for sessions on request.

Playa del Carmen — marina blue and street register

Playa del Carmen is a different register from its southern neighbours. The marina, the ferry pier, the white-furniture beach clubs and the Fifth Avenue corridor read marina blue at mid-morning and editorial contrast at sunset. We use Playa for sessions that want resort architecture in the frame rather than open coast — Mamitas Beach for late-afternoon wide compositions, the marina for portraits with boats and stone, Calle Corazón for the street register. It is the strongest "town" location on the corridor.

Mayakoba — open shade, fully composed

Mayakoba is the most architecturally directed location in the Riviera Maya. The four properties — Rosewood, Andaz, Banyan Tree, Fairmont — sit inside a single landscape of jungle waterways and lagoon walkways. The register is almost entirely open-shade soft: the canopy is heavy, the water is still, the timber walkways double the frame in reflection. Rosewood Mayakoba is our first recommendation for editorial couples sessions and small destination weddings; Andaz for the contemporary, graphic-architecture brief; Banyan Tree for villa-private hours. Mayakoba is the corridor's strongest "interior" location even though most of it is technically outside.

Maroma — quiet golden hour

The Maroma stretch — Belmond Maroma, Banyan Tree Mayakoba's beach access, the resorts north of Playa del Carmen — is the corridor's quietest golden-hour beach. Wide, white, protected from the wind by the reef line. The register is golden-hour warm, but the light at Maroma sits a half-stop cooler than Tulum because of the angle of the coastline. We schedule Maroma for sunrise sessions — the property orientation favours the morning side — and pair it with an interior hour at the resort itself in the late afternoon.

How we plan a corridor day

A typical Riviera Maya session day is built in three blocks: a sunrise block on east-facing beach (Tulum, Akumal, Maroma), an open-shade block in the resort interior between four-thirty and six (Mayakoba, the Tulum boutiques), and an editorial-contrast insert at a cenote between eleven and one. We do not work the open-beach hours between eleven and three unless the brief specifically demands them.

For destination wedding couples, we often add a second day for the marina-blue register — a Playa del Carmen morning, or a private yacht hour off the coast.

Wardrobe, by register

Each register asks for a different palette. Golden-hour warm takes ivory, sand, terracotta, cognac. Open-shade soft takes the same palette plus deeper greens and slate. Marina blue takes white, navy, Atlantic stripe, raw linen. Editorial contrast takes black, deep cream, anything the cenote light can sculpt. Linen and silk over cotton-jersey, always. The full palette guide is in our outfit guide.

Service detail

We work the full corridor — Tulum, Akumal, Playa del Carmen, Mayakoba, Maroma. The Riviera Maya destination page covers session structure, what each property allows, and how we coordinate access. For session inquiries: send your travel dates, the resort, and one or two reference frames. We respond inside twenty-four hours.
