The all-inclusive resort photographer, honestly.

The all-inclusive model is built around the resort. Editorial photography is built around the light. Here is how IVAE Studios works inside that tension — concierge coordination, vendor passes, and the honest difference between a house photographer and an editorial-independent studio.

April 2, 2026 · ~8 min read · By Vianey Ortega

The all-inclusive resort is one of the most successful hospitality formats ever invented. Wristband on, decisions off. The food, the drinks, the entertainment, the staff, the photography — everything pre-packaged and pre-paid so the guest does not have to think. That is the appeal, and it works. It works for a hundred different reasons. It does not, however, work for editorial photography. Not because the resorts are bad — many are excellent — but because the model is built around volume and convenience, and editorial work is built around restraint and time.

This piece is the version of the conversation we have on the planning call when a client asks the right question: do we just use the resort photographer or do we book you? The honest answer has more nuance than either side of the marketing usually admits. Below is how IVAE Studios works inside the all-inclusive system, what the in-house team is actually good at, and which sessions belong to which photographer.

How the all-inclusive model handles photography

Almost every luxury all-inclusive in Mexico runs photography one of three ways. First, an in-house team contracted exclusively to the property — uniformed, trained on the resort's brand, present at every wedding and most photo opportunities. Second, an external photo concession that the resort sells to the guest as part of the package. Third, an open-vendor model where guests bring their own studio and the resort coordinates access. Many properties run a hybrid: in-house for the brochure-style content and the welcome package, open-vendor for the editorial sessions guests actively choose to book.

Each of those models exists for a reason. The in-house team produces consistent, on-brand imagery for the resort itself. The concession photographer produces volume at a price point. The outside studio produces editorial. They are not competing for the same job — they are different jobs that look superficially similar.

The resort is the location. The light is the deadline. The photographer is the third decision.

House photographer vs editorial-independent studio

The honest difference is not skill. There are extraordinarily talented house photographers in Mexico. The honest difference is incentive. A house photographer's job is to deliver hundreds of sessions a year on a brand-controlled aesthetic that satisfies a wide range of tastes. An editorial-independent studio's job is to deliver one session that looks like nothing else, made for one family.

Three things follow from that. First, scheduling. The house team typically works the hours the resort assigns — often the convenient mid-day blocks that fit in between dinner services and ceremony rehearsals. An editorial studio works the hours the light dictates, which is generally outside those blocks. Second, location. The house team works the spaces the resort wants featured: the new pool deck, the renovated lobby, the recently refurbished cabana. An editorial studio works the spaces that produce the strongest frames on that specific date for that specific family — sometimes the lobby, often the lagoon side that no marketing department photographs. Third, style. The house team is held to a brand-safe aesthetic. An editorial studio is held to a singular point of view.

None of that makes one wrong. It makes them different products. We tell clients to use the in-house team for the welcome dinner, the rehearsal, the family group shot at check-in. We ask to be the studio for the session itself.

How IVAE coordinates with the concierge

The mechanics of bringing an outside studio into an all-inclusive resort are not complicated — they are just unfamiliar to most guests. Here is the actual workflow we run for every session, end to end.

Two to four weeks before the trip

We email the resort concierge or the wedding coordinator with the session date, the guest's reservation name and the property locations we are planning to use. We ask for confirmation in writing of the vendor access fee, any wristband or badge requirements, and the resort's quiet rules around tripods, lighting and drones. We also confirm whether the property charges the photographer or the guest — both are common, and the answer changes the invoice.

One week before

We send the call sheet to the client and a short version to the concierge. The concierge version lists the photographer's full name and ID number for the security gate, the time block we will be on property, and the points of contact on our side. The client version includes the meeting time, the locations, the wardrobe note, and the weather contingency.

Day of the session

We arrive thirty minutes before call time. The security gate has the name. The wristband or vendor pass is collected. We meet the client at the lobby or the agreed location. The session runs sixty to ninety minutes. The wristband goes back to the desk on the way out. The concierge gets a thank-you note within twenty-four hours; the client gets a preview within seventy-two.

None of those steps are visible to the client during their stay. That is the point. The all-inclusive is supposed to feel frictionless — an outside studio that does its own paperwork keeps it that way.

The vendor pass, in plain numbers

Vendor access fees at luxury Mexican all-inclusives generally run between fifty and three hundred US dollars for portrait sessions, and higher for weddings — sometimes up to a thousand for full-day wedding coverage. Some properties waive the fee entirely for in-house guests booking sessions. A few of the larger brands include the fee in their existing wedding packages but charge separately for portrait work. We confirm the exact number for your property and date during the planning call so there are no surprises on either invoice.

Our pricing presents the vendor fee separately when the resort charges it. We do not mark it up or hide it inside our own line items. If your resort waives it, you do not pay it. If your resort charges three hundred, you see three hundred.

Which sessions belong to which photographer

Use the house team for

The welcome arrival photo at the lobby. The big multi-generational group shot at the rehearsal dinner. Reception coverage that overlaps with the resort's own event documentation. Anything that has to be turned around the same evening. These are the strengths of an in-house operation: presence, speed, and brand-safe coverage of an event the resort is already staffing.

Use IVAE for

The editorial portrait session. The pre-wedding couple's session. The family session that needs to look like an heirloom in fifteen years, not a vacation snapshot. The proposal. The sessions where the location, the light and the wardrobe were planned in advance and the gallery is the deliverable. Wedding day editorial direction, where the document-everything coverage is handled by another team and we are there to make twenty extraordinary frames instead of two thousand adequate ones.

What we ask the resort never to do

One quiet line we always set with the concierge. Do not have an in-house photographer cover the same hour we are working. The mistake is benign — the resort wants to support the guest experience — but two photographers in the same beach window means two different aesthetics, two sets of cues for the family, and a session that splits its energy. We ask for the hour exclusively. We have never been refused.
