Journal · Engagement

The morning before the dinner. The session after the yes.

The engagement session is deliberate work, not a surprise. Two people who already know the answer, one hour of editorial direction at the resort they're already in.

April 2, 2026 · ~6 min read · By Vianey Ortega

Engagement sessions are the most-requested format we shoot, and almost none of them look like the version that runs in stock photos. There is no ring-pop, no fake-shocked expression, no photographer hiding behind a palm tree. The session is scheduled, the wardrobe is considered, and both people know exactly what's about to happen. The work begins after that decision, not at it.

For the surprise itself — the proposal, the reaction, the genuinely-private moment — we run a different brief entirely. That's its own field of work, and it deserves its own piece. This one is about everything that happens before the proposal, or the morning after.

Two situations, one format

The morning before the dinner

The most common request. The proposal will happen at dinner; the session is at golden hour the same day, before either person is in the dress or the suit. The pretext is "let's get a couples session in while we're here." The truth is that one of them already has the ring in a hotel safe, and the photographs of them on the beach an hour earlier — laughing, walking, slightly windblown — become the strongest pre-engagement portraits the relationship will ever produce, simply because they were taken in that emotional state.

The session immediately after the proposal

Some couples decide together to be photographed. The yes happens in private — at sunrise, on a balcony, on a quiet stretch of beach — and the session begins forty minutes later, while the emotion is still uncensored. This is not surprise photography. The proposing partner has already asked, the answering partner has already answered, and we walk in to document the relationship as it now exists, not as it just changed.

Both formats are deliberate. Both are scheduled in advance. Neither asks anyone to fake a reaction.

Direction is not staging. The first is precision; the second is theatre.

What we direct (and what we don't)

The light is non-negotiable. We shoot the seventy minutes around sunset on the Caribbean side and the same window on the Pacific in Los Cabos. The wardrobe gets one page of guidance ahead of the session — palette, fabric, what photographs against the resort. The location is mapped: which corner of the beach at which minute, which terrace catches the back-light, which path is empty during dinner service.

What we don't direct is anything resembling a pose. Direction is conversational — keep walking, finish the sentence, look at her instead of me. Couples who have read every Pinterest board on the topic almost always abandon the script in the first ten minutes, and the work gets stronger the moment they do.

Why the session is worth more than save-the-dates

Couples often book the session as a save-the-date production line — twenty good portraits to choose from, one of them ends up on a card. That's a fine outcome and a real budget reason to do the work. But it's the smaller half of why the session matters.

The larger half: an engagement session is the rehearsal before the wedding day. By the time we're shooting your destination wedding a year later, you've been directed by the same photographer once already. The first ten minutes — the part where most couples are stiff and self-conscious — has already happened. On the wedding day we walk in already two-thirds of the way to comfortable.

Where it works best

Riviera Maya

The strongest landscape for an editorial session. Resort beaches dressed up by hotel architecture, jungle-framed cenotes for couples who want a second register, and the option to step away from the resort entirely for a quiet stretch of Tulum or Akumal coastline.

Cancún

Hotel Zone resorts face directly east, which means a backlit golden-hour beach with no harsh sun in either face. The Cancún resort corridor also has the cleanest logistics: most properties allow a session on their own beach with a single concierge call, and the architecture between buildings reads as cinematic without effort.

Los Cabos

For couples who want sunset over the ocean rather than over the resort. Los Cabos trades open shade and turquoise for high contrast and amber light dropping into the Pacific. A different aesthetic, and the strongest single setting in Mexico for a sunset silhouette engagement frame.

How a typical session runs

Sixty to ninety minutes, ending into blue hour. We meet forty minutes before the light peaks, walk the location with you, and shoot continuously rather than stopping for resets. Outfits are already on; hair is already done. The first fifteen minutes are warm-up — the strongest work happens between minute twenty and minute fifty, when both people have stopped performing for the camera and started talking to each other again.

The gallery is delivered hand-culled and color-graded across the set in 1–3 business days. Many couples have their save-the-date stack before they fly home.
