How to Plan a Luxury Destination Wedding in Cancún and the Riviera Maya

Where to marry, which resort, a legal or symbolic ceremony, the best months, the full timeline, and how to bring everyone there. An honest, practical guide from the studio that photographs this coastline year-round.

Why This Coastline

A wedding that travels beautifully

A destination wedding in Cancún and the Riviera Maya is not one afternoon. It is a long weekend, sometimes a full week, in which guests fly in from several countries, the days fill with welcome dinners and beach ceremonies and farewell brunches, and a stretch of Caribbean coastline becomes the backdrop for the most photographed days of your life. This part of Mexico has spent decades becoming the most practiced destination-wedding region in the Americas, which is exactly why it works: the airport is large, the resorts are deep in experience, and the light, for most of the year, is extraordinary.

This guide is written by Vianey Díaz and the team at IVAE Studios, a luxury destination photography studio based in Cancún. We photograph weddings across this coast every season, so we have watched what makes a celebration run smoothly and what creates avoidable stress. The pages that follow are organized in the order you will actually face the decisions: choosing where, choosing the resort, deciding how to marry, picking the season, building the timeline, and getting everyone there. It is reference material, not a sales pitch. Where a number would be invented we have kept it qualitative on purpose, because every wedding budgets differently.

Step One

Choosing the region

The first decision shapes everything after it. The five areas couples most often weigh sit within a couple of hours of each other on the Yucatán Peninsula, except Los Cabos, which is a different coast entirely. Each has a distinct character, density and look.

01

Cancún

The most convenient choice. The largest airport in the region sits minutes away, the resort density is highest, and the Hotel Zone offers a long Caribbean beach with the widest range of large all-inclusive properties. Best for groups who value short transfers and lots of room options.

CharacterLively, accessible, beach-classic
02

Riviera Maya

South of Cancún through Playa del Carmen, Mayakoba and Akumal. You trade a little drive time for lower density, lush jungle landscaping, lagoon estates and access to cenotes. Home to some of the most refined resorts on the coast and a favorite for couples wanting a quieter, greener setting.

CharacterLush, refined, low-density
03

Playa Mujeres

Just north of Cancún, newer and very polished. Playa Mujeres and Costa Mujeres are calmer and more exclusive than the Hotel Zone, with wide beaches that are far less prone to sargassum because they face north and west. Strong for couples who want luxury without the crowds.

CharacterNew, exclusive, calm water
04

Tulum

The most design-forward and bohemian. Jungle-and-beach venues, boho-luxe boutique hotels and beach clubs with a distinct aesthetic. Transfers are longer and the strip is less polished than the resorts up north, but nothing else looks quite like a Tulum wedding.

CharacterBoho-luxe, design-led, intimate
05

Los Cabos

A different coast: the Pacific tip of Baja California Sur, with its own international airport. Desert-meets-sea drama, dramatic rock formations, almost no sargassum, and a cooler, drier feel. Choose Cabo when you want a landscape that reads nothing like the Caribbean.

CharacterDesert-sea, dramatic, sargassum-free
06

How to decide

Weigh transfer time from the airport, the look you want in photos, how exposed the beach is to sargassum, and how many guests you expect. Convenience favors Cancún and Playa Mujeres; design favors Tulum; refinement favors the Riviera Maya; a non-Caribbean look favors Cabo. There is no wrong answer, only the right fit for your group.

WeighTransfers, look, sargassum, group size
Step Two

Choosing the resort or venue

Once the region is set, the venue is the single biggest decision you will make, because it determines your budget structure, your vendor freedom and how many of your celebration's events can happen in one place.

All-inclusive or boutique

Both can be genuinely luxurious; they solve different problems. An all-inclusive resort bundles rooms, food, drink and often a wedding package under one roof. That simplifies guest logistics and budgeting enormously, which is why many of the region's most polished properties are all-inclusive and why large groups gravitate to them. A boutique hotel, private villa or standalone venue gives you more design freedom, a more personal feel and usually a more open vendor policy, in exchange for more coordination and separate catering. Neither is better in the abstract; the right answer depends on your guest count and how hands-on you want to be.

The three questions that matter most

Whatever the style, ask the same three things early. First, the food-and-beverage minimum and what is included, because this is often the real floor of the budget. Second, how many events the venue can host across your weekend, since a welcome dinner, ceremony, reception and farewell brunch in the same place is far easier than moving the group around. Third, and most overlooked, the outside-vendor policy.

Outside-vendor policies and fees

Many resorts maintain a preferred or in-house list of photographers, florists, DJs and planners, and some charge an outside-vendor fee, a day pass, or a vendor-access fee to bring in a creative team of your own choosing. Policies vary widely from open to fairly restrictive, and the details change, so confirm them in writing before you sign. If photography matters to you, it is worth choosing a venue whose policy lets you hire the photographer you love rather than the one assigned to the property. A good destination wedding photographer can usually advise on which venues are flexible, because they work across many of them.

Kosher-capable and specialty venues

If your celebration needs specific catering or ritual support, raise it during the venue search, not after. Several resorts and planners in the region have experience hosting Jewish destination weddings, and kosher catering generally requires advance arrangement with a specialist caterer, supervision, and a koshered or dedicated kitchen. The same logic applies to Hindu, Sikh and other South Asian and faith traditions: the venues that can accommodate them well are a smaller list, so build it first. You can explore properties on our venues overview, which covers many of the resorts couples shortlist.

Step Three

Legal or symbolic ceremony

One of the most common questions, and the one most couples get wrong order on. In Mexico you can have a symbolic ceremony, a legal civil wedding, or a religious ceremony, and they carry very different requirements. Here is the practical difference. Always confirm the specifics with your planner, since rules vary by state and change over time.

Symbolic ceremony

The most popular choice for travelers. There is no legal paperwork, which gives you total freedom over the script, the officiant, the vows, the timing and the setting on the beach. Most couples sign the legal marriage privately at home, often a quick civil signing days before or after the trip, and treat the destination ceremony as the real celebration.

Legal civil wedding

A legally binding marriage performed in Mexico by a civil judge. It is entirely possible but adds requirements such as translated and apostilled documents, identification, witnesses, and in some states a blood test and a local residency window of a few days. Couples who want it usually start the paperwork well in advance through their planner.

Religious ceremony

Requirements depend on the faith and the officiant. Catholic, Jewish, and other religious weddings each have their own preparation, and many couples bring an officiant who travels in or work with a local one the venue recommends. Some religious ceremonies can also be made legal; many couples keep the legal step separate.

The simplest path

For most international couples, the cleanest plan is a symbolic ceremony in Mexico plus a separate legal marriage at home. It removes a layer of paperwork, translation and timing risk from the trip and lets the beach ceremony be purely about the moment. If a legal Mexican wedding matters to you, decide early so the documents are ready.

Step Four

Best months, weather and sargassum

Timing affects your photos, your guests' comfort and your budget. The Caribbean coast of Mexico has a clear seasonal rhythm worth planning around.

The dry season is the safe bet

The most reliable window runs roughly November through April, with December through March the calmest and clearest. These months bring lower humidity, steady trade winds and the best water clarity of the year, which is exactly why they are also peak wedding season and the most in-demand dates. If you want a Saturday in this window, plan early.

The shoulder window

Late April, May and early June are an underrated stretch: warm, still mostly dry, with fewer crowds and softer pricing before the summer heat and afternoon rains build. The light along the coast remains beautiful, and venues and vendors have more availability. The Atlantic hurricane season technically opens in June and runs through November, so couples marrying in summer or early autumn usually build in flexibility and travel insurance.

Understanding sargassum

Sargassum is a brown seaweed that can drift onto Caribbean-facing beaches, generally heaviest from roughly April through August and highly variable year to year. Many resorts run active cleanup and offshore-barrier programs, and the north and west-facing coasts, such as Playa Mujeres, Isla Mujeres and the inland cenotes, are far less affected, as is Los Cabos on the Pacific. It rarely disrupts a wedding outright, but during the peak months it is worth asking your venue about their sargassum management and keeping a covered or garden ceremony option in reserve.

Plan the day around the light

Whatever the month, the best portrait light is the last hour to ninety minutes before sunset, when the sea turns warm and the crowds thin. Sunset shifts by more than an hour across the year, so set your ceremony time by the actual sunset for your date and place the most important photographs in that window. Your photographer can build the whole timeline backward from it.

Step Five

Building the timeline

A destination wedding rewards early, orderly planning. Here is the sequence most couples follow, from the first save-the-date to the wedding day itself.

12 to 18 months

Lock the foundation

Set the budget, choose the region and the date, and book the venue. Send save-the-dates as early as you can so guests can plan flights and rooms, and secure the vendors you most want, starting with the planner, photographer and videographer.

6 to 9 months

Build the celebration

Confirm the room block, finalize florals, entertainment and décor, plan the welcome and farewell events, and decide on a symbolic or legal ceremony so any paperwork can begin. Launch a simple wedding website with travel details.

2 to 4 months

Confirm the details

Send formal invitations, collect RSVPs, finalize the menu and the guest count, arrange transfers, and walk the timeline with your planner and photographer so golden hour and the key moments are protected.

Wedding week

Arrive and celebrate

Most couples and close family arrive a few days early. The welcome dinner gathers everyone, the rehearsal settles the ceremony, the wedding day runs to the timeline, and a farewell brunch closes the trip the morning after.

Step Six

Planning the multi-day celebration

Because everyone has traveled, a destination wedding is rarely a single event. The strongest weekends are designed as an arc of gatherings, each with its own purpose.

The welcome dinner

The first evening, usually the night before or two nights before the wedding, is when the guest group meets. A beach club, rooftop or open-air restaurant works beautifully, and it does more than feed everyone: it breaks the ice so that by the wedding day the room already feels like one party. Couples often photograph this evening too, since it captures the candid energy of the trip.

The rehearsal and ceremony day

A short rehearsal, often the afternoon before, settles the processional and timing. The wedding day itself is built around the light: getting-ready coverage, the ceremony placed near golden hour, portraits in that warm window, then the reception into the evening. A clear, realistic timeline, made with your planner and photographer, is what keeps the day calm.

The farewell brunch

A relaxed brunch the morning after lets everyone say goodbye before they scatter to flights. It is the gentlest possible end to a weekend of celebration and, for many couples, one of the most photographed and emotional moments of the trip. If you are covering several of these events, our destination wedding coverage is designed to span the full weekend.

Step Seven

When to book photo and film

The single most repeated piece of advice from couples who have done this: book your photographer and videographer early, right after the date and venue are confirmed.

The strongest destination photographers and videographers take a limited number of weddings per season, and the best Saturdays in high season are claimed first, often twelve months out or more. Booking early does more than reserve the date: it means your photographer can help shape the timeline around the light, so the portraits that matter most land in the golden-hour window rather than getting squeezed. A destination wedding photographer who lives on this coast is already past the learning curve a fly-in vendor faces, knows the venues, and adds no destination travel fee for weddings in the region.

If you want both stills and film, secure a coordinated photo-and-film team early so the two are in sync on the day rather than competing for the same moments. Our Cancún wedding videography is planned alongside the photography for exactly that reason. Whoever you choose, ask to see full galleries from real weddings at venues like yours, confirm coverage hours and deliverables in writing, and make sure their schedule and yours are locked before you finalize other vendors.

A short note on budget

Costs vary widely by guest count, season, venue and how many days you celebrate, so it helps to budget by component rather than chase one figure. The largest line items are typically the venue and food-and-beverage minimum, the room block, and the creative team of planner, photographer, videographer, florals and entertainment. Add outside-vendor fees, taxes and service charges, welcome and farewell events, and décor and lighting. Keep a contingency for currency movement and last-minute guest changes, and ask every vendor for a written, itemized quote so you are comparing like for like. Step Eight

Getting your guests there

A destination wedding asks more of your guests than a hometown one, so the kindest thing you can do is make their travel effortless. Cancún is unusually well connected, which helps.

Flights from the US, Canada, Mexico City and Europe

Cancún International Airport is one of the busiest in Latin America, with direct flights from dozens of US and Canadian cities, frequent service from Mexico City and other Mexican hubs, and seasonal direct routes from several European cities, with easy one-stop connections otherwise. Guests from the US and Canada often have a nonstop option; guests from Mexico City are a short hop away; guests from Europe should book earlier and may connect through a US or Mexican gateway. Los Cabos has its own international airport with strong US and Canadian connections.

Documents, transfers and the welcome event

US, Canadian and EU passport holders generally do not need a tourist visa for Mexico, but ask every guest to confirm their own passport validity and entry requirements well ahead. Set up a room block, share clear arrival instructions and arrange airport transfers, ideally grouped, so nobody is stranded. Because guests land on different flights across the day, a welcome event the first evening is the simplest way to gather everyone and let the celebration begin the moment they arrive.

A wedding website earns its keep

A single page with the schedule, the resort, travel tips, the dress code and an RSVP saves countless individual questions and reassures guests who have never traveled to Mexico. Pair it with save-the-dates sent as early as possible, and your guest experience is most of the way solved.

A Note On

Jewish, kosher and cultural weddings

Faith and cultural traditions are entirely at home on this coast, but they reward earlier and more specific planning than a standard celebration.

The region has real experience with Jewish destination weddings. A number of resorts and planners can accommodate the elements a traditional celebration needs: a chuppah on the beach, a tisch and bedeken, the ketubah signing, the hora, and coordination with an officiating rabbi who travels in. Kosher catering generally requires advance arrangement, often with a specialist caterer, mashgiach supervision and a koshered or dedicated kitchen, which is why it should be one of the first questions when you shortlist venues rather than a late addition.

The same advance-planning logic applies to other traditions, including Hindu, Sikh and South Asian ceremonies, multi-day celebrations with mehndi and sangeet, and any wedding with specific ritual, music or catering needs. The venues that handle these well are a smaller, more specialized list, so identify them first and build the rest of the plan around them. A photographer who has covered these celebrations before will understand the rhythm and the moments that matter, which is part of why we keep dedicated guidance for couples planning a cultural wedding on this coast.
