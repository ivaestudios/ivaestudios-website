Journal · Family

The resort family session, composed around your youngest child.

How we shape a destination family shoot — resort discipline, light read, pacing — so the gallery feels considered rather than corralled.

April 2026 · 9 min read · By Vianey Ortega

Most resort family photos look the same because nothing about them was decided. Vacation arrives, an hour opens up, somebody points a camera. The gallery that comes home is a record of a holiday — not a record of a family. The destination family session is a different proposition. It begins, weeks before anyone packs, with three deliberate choices: the resort, the light, and the child whose energy will set the pace.

The resort decides more than you think

A family session at a luxury resort is a session inside someone else's architecture. The orientation of the beach, the position of the pier, the height of the palm canopy, the colour of the umbrellas the property uses that season — all of it is on the photograph before anyone says smile. We choose locations against the resort's natural lines: a quiet stretch beyond the towel cart for the wide pulls, a covered colonnade for open shade when the children need a break, the pier at last light for the only frame that needs to be shot tight.

That research happens before you arrive. We confirm vendor access with the concierge, walk the beach in our heads against the date's tide and azimuth, and lock the session window before anything else is decided. By the time you meet us at the lobby, the only variable left is the family.

Golden hour is non-negotiable

The Caribbean and the Pacific each give roughly seventy minutes of usable directional light per day. Outside that window, the sun sits high and the photographs flatten. We schedule sessions to begin twenty minutes before the gold register starts and end as the sky turns from amber to ink. The children get the easy half, when light is warm and the air is cool. The couple gets the last ten minutes, when the beach empties and the camera moves close.

The sun never waits for a tantrum. The session does. The gallery is what survives the difference.

Pacing to the youngest child

A session built around an eight-year-old is a different session than one built around a toddler. We pace to the youngest. That means a real snack break at minute twenty-five, a pause for water that becomes a portrait of two parents while the kids decompress, and a refusal to stretch the session past the point where the children stop trusting the camera. Sixty to ninety minutes is the working window for nearly every family. Anything longer is bargaining.

Wardrobe is a single conversation, not five. Coordinate in earth and resort whites — ivory, sand, cream, slate, soft rust — without matching. Children in linen and cotton they can run in. No logos, no graphic prints, no neon. Bare feet on the beach, a straw hat for one of you, nothing accessorised past the point of intention. The outfit guide we send is short on purpose.

The three coastlines, briefly

Riviera Maya gives the widest variety in a single day — beach, jungle, cenote, ruined wall — but it costs travel time, which costs children. Cancún is the cleanest logistical win: the Hotel Zone keeps everything inside one car ride, and the open-shade structure of resorts like Nizuc, Rosewood Mayakoba and Atelier Playa Mujeres holds up at every hour we've worked. Los Cabos is the Pacific, drier and more sculptural, with desert light that produces saturated portraits and silhouettes against El Arco that no Caribbean session can match.

We choose between them on the call, after we know the resort, the dates and the children's ages. There is no universal answer. There is the right answer for your family, this trip.

What you bring

A small bag. Water for everyone, a snack the youngest already eats, one comfort object per young child. Hair and makeup finished forty-five minutes before call time so nothing is being adjusted on location. Sunscreen applied two hours before, so it has absorbed and stopped catching highlight. Phones away — yours and ours.

Everything else is our job. Direction, framing, light read, pacing, the conversation with the resort if the chairs aren't where they should be. Your job is the family, not the camera. The gallery you receive in three to five days is the part that travels home with you.
