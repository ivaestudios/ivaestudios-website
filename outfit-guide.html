Outfit Guide · IVAE Studios

What to wear, by light register.

Editorial photographs depend as much on what's worn as on the light. This is the guide we send paying clients — open to anyone planning a session in Riviera Maya, Cancún or Los Cabos.

Tailored guidance · 6 session types

The shape of your session.

Each session asks something slightly different of the wardrobe. Pick yours.

Family · 2–7 people

Coordinated, never matched.

A family in identical white shirts photographs flat. We compose a palette of two or three tones — let each person pick a piece inside it. Solids photograph richer than prints.

  • Palette of three. Choose two anchor tones (one warm, one cool) and one accent. Distribute across the group.
  • Light fabrics. Linen, gauze, cotton — anything that catches a breeze on the beach.
  • Footwear. Barefoot is best on sand. White shoes turn beige in five minutes.
  • Newest walkers. A neutral romper photographs better than a bright graphic onesie.
  • Layers. One textured layer per adult — a linen jacket, a soft cardigan — for compositional weight.

Quick read

  • Two anchor tones · one accent
  • Solids over prints
  • Linen, gauze, cotton
  • Barefoot on sand
  • No coordinated hats

Couples · 2 people

Contrast within the frame.

The strongest couples portraits hold one darker silhouette against one lighter — the eye reads the pair as a composition, not a duplicate. Movement-friendly fabrics let the wind do the work.

  • Tonal contrast. One person in a darker register (Atlantic Navy, Soft Slate), one in a lighter one (Resort White, Warm Sand).
  • Movement fabric. A flowing dress, a wide trouser leg — anything that responds to wind.
  • His piece. Linen pants, chambray shirt, leather sandal. Roll the sleeves.
  • Her piece. A long dress in a single tone reads better than a printed shorter one.
  • Jewelry. Gold over silver against warm Caribbean light.

Quick read

  • One darker · one lighter
  • Movement-friendly fabric
  • Linen + chambray for him
  • Long dress, single tone
  • Gold jewelry, not silver

Weddings · multi-event

Three palettes for one day.

A destination wedding is rarely one outfit. We help you build three palettes — ceremony, reception, the morning after — that read as a single editorial story across the gallery.

  • Ceremony. Bridal canon — but think about the venue. Beach white runs warm; chapel white runs cool.
  • Reception. Warm white with gold accents, or a deeper second-look dress for night portraits.
  • Bridal party. One palette across the party — let each person pick the silhouette inside it.
  • Family attire. Coordinate the parents' tones to the bridal party — not to the bride.
  • Welcome dinner. Resort-formal: linen suit, long sundress. The lighter, the better for sunset.

Quick read

  • Three palettes, one story
  • Warm whites at sunset
  • Gold accents · not silver
  • Parents tone to party
  • Plan a second-look dress

Maternity · 32–36 weeks

The silhouette follows the bump.

Tight-at-the-waist photographs as costume. Body-skimming, never body-clinging — a long skin-tone slip, an open robe, a sheer overlay. The silhouette honors the shape rather than constricting it.

  • Flowing silhouette. A long slip or empire-waist dress reads timeless. A tight bodycon reads dated.
  • Skin tones. Cream, sand, soft slate — neutrals that make the bump the subject, not the dress.
  • Texture. Sheer chiffon, washed silk, ribbed knit. A single textured piece adds editorial weight.
  • Partner. Matched palette, never matched outfit. He: linen shirt in the same family, sleeves rolled.
  • Older child. Soft neutral — a romper or simple dress that doesn't pull focus from the bump.

Quick read

  • Body-skimming, not clinging
  • Skin-tone neutrals
  • One textured piece
  • Partner: matched palette
  • Avoid tight waist seams

Yacht · half-day charter

Nautical, never costume.

The cliché is white pants with brass buttons. The editorial answer is restrained: linen, sand, navy — the palette of the boat itself. Let the deck and the horizon do the work.

  • Palette of the marina. Resort white, atlantic navy, warm sand. Three tones across the group.
  • Linen pants. Wide-leg, rolled hem, barefoot. Always.
  • Avoid loose hats. The wind on the bow is real. A fitted straw hat or no hat.
  • Swimwear. One-piece in a solid tone reads cleaner than a printed bikini.
  • Sunglasses. Tortoise or warm metal — never neon plastic.

Quick read

  • White · navy · sand
  • Linen pants, rolled
  • Fitted hat or none
  • Solid swimwear
  • Tortoise sunglasses

Cenote · 60–90 minutes

The light is green-blue. Plan for it.

Cenote light reads cool and saturated — black, deep emerald, terracotta and skin-tone neutrals all photograph beautifully. Bright primary colors look synthetic against the cave water.

  • Swimwear-only. The water is the wardrobe. A black or earth-tone one-piece is the clearest editorial choice.
  • Quick-dry minimal. Some galleries call for a long flowing piece in the water — choose a quick-dry gauze, not heavy cotton.
  • Hair on entry. Dry. Hair on exit becomes part of the gallery — slick-back wet looks deliberate.
  • No metallic finishes. Sequins and chrome don't read in cave light.
  • Towel and a robe. For transitions between portraits — and for the drive back.

Quick read

  • Black · earth · skin tones
  • Quick-dry, not cotton
  • Dry hair on entry
  • No sequins, no chrome
  • Bring a robe

By location

The light changes. So should the wardrobe.

A beach golden hour and a cenote interior ask for almost opposite palettes. A short note on each.

Beach · golden hour

Warm light, light fabrics.

The hour before sunset turns sand to copper. Resort White and Warm Sand palettes glow; cool blues recede. Pick fabrics that catch wind — linen, gauze, sheer cotton.

  • Resort White or Warm Sand palette
  • Linen, gauze, sheer cotton
  • Barefoot on sand — always
  • Avoid white shoes (they turn beige)

Resort interior

Open shade, structured pieces.

Hotel courtyards, lobbies, and arched colonnades favor structure — a tailored linen suit, a fitted slip, a single accessory. Resort architecture deserves a wardrobe with edges.

  • Atlantic Navy or Resort White palette
  • Tailored linen, structured silk
  • Polished sandal or loafer
  • One bag, one watch — restraint

Cenote · interior light

Cool water, deep tones.

Cenote light reads green-blue and saturated. Black and deep earth tones photograph beautifully; bright primary colors look synthetic. Plan the entry — dry hair and a clean towel are part of the kit.

  • Editorial Contrast palette
  • Black or earth-tone swimwear
  • Quick-dry overlay if any
  • Robe + towel for transitions

Yacht · marina light

Atlantic blue, restrained palette.

The deck reads as a stage; the wardrobe should be the marina itself. Resort White, Atlantic Navy and Warm Sand — three tones across the group. The wind is real; plan for it.

  • Atlantic Navy palette · 3 tones
  • Linen pants, rolled hem
  • Fitted hat or none
  • Solid swimwear over print

The list we hand at the consult

Avoid these. With reasoning.

Twelve items we've watched derail otherwise excellent sessions. Each carries a one-sentence why.

  1. Logos and graphic prints

    They pull the eye. We compose around faces — a brand name on a shirt becomes the new subject.

  2. Neon and electric brights

    Saturated reds, hot pinks and lime greens reflect onto skin and warm light alike — they read synthetic on camera.

  3. Tight waist seams on maternity

    A horizontal seam under the bump cuts the silhouette. Empire-waist or skin-tone slip every time.

  4. Matching outfits, identical pieces

    Five identical white shirts photograph as a uniform. We coordinate palettes, not pieces.

  5. Brand-new shoes on sand

    Sand discolors leather and white sole within five minutes. Barefoot is the editorial default.

  6. Fragile tan lines visible

    Sharp lines from a different swimsuit show under shoulder straps and skim necklines. Even out the day before.

  7. Loose-brim hats in wind

    A floppy hat lifts off. A fitted straw or boater stays. Or no hat — your hair becomes the gesture.

  8. Heavy makeup at golden hour

    The light is already warm. A heavy bronzer plus warm light reads orange. Strip it back by half.

  9. Sunglasses worn full-frame

    They cover the most expressive feature. Use them as a hand prop, on the head, or off entirely.

  10. Silver and chrome accessories

    Cool metal fights warm Caribbean light. Gold, brass and tortoise read warmer in the gallery.

  11. Wrinkled linen, packed flat

    Linen wrinkles are part of its charm only at the right scale. Hang the night before; steam at the resort.

  12. Different undertones across the group

    A cream-white plus a blue-white reads as a mistake on camera. Pick one undertone family and stay in it.
