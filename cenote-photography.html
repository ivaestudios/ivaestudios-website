The session

How a cenote session is built.

What we plan

Before the morning of

  • Cenote selection. We pick based on light angle that week, water clarity reports, and the hour the crowd arrives. Three options shortlisted, one confirmed.
  • Light gear. Underwater housing, a single off-camera fill, and the cenote's natural sun shaft — never a flash if the cenote forbids it.
  • Swimwear and wardrobe guide. Fabric weight, color register, what holds shape underwater. Shared the week prior.
  • Contingency. If clarity drops the morning of, we move to the secondary cenote on the shortlist. The session does not get rushed; it gets relocated.
What we deliver

After the session

  • Edited gallery in 1–3 days for a curated preview, full set inside two weeks. No watermark, no proof grids.
  • Color graded for the cenote register. Cool greens and blues anchored, skin warmed back to natural — the blue-green cast that marks an unfinished cenote file is corrected, not preserved.
  • Above-water and underwater frames in the same edit pass, so the gallery reads as a single sequence, not two shoots stitched together.
  • Print-ready files at full resolution. The cenote is a print piece — the best images live on a wall, not a feed.
Where we shoot

Three cenotes, three registers.

Tulum holds dozens of cenotes worth photographing. We work with three regularly because they cover the full range of light, depth, and intimacy a session needs.

i.

Cenote Gran

Tulum · 4 km from town

The widest cavern of the three. A late-morning sun shaft cuts the chamber for a narrow window each day — we book that window. Wooden platform descent, stalactites overhead, deep water at the centre. Strongest single location for half-and-half compositions.

ii.

Cenote Dos Ojos

22 km north on Hwy 307

Two connected pools, exceptional clarity, popular with day-trippers. We book the first slot of the day, before the snorkel groups arrive — the room belongs to the session for roughly an hour. Best for fully submerged underwater portraits.

iii.

Cenote Calavera

Tulum · small & intimate

Three openings in the limestone — the local name is Temple of Doom. Smaller, quieter, late-afternoon light angles down through the holes. The most editorial of the three: smaller frame, harder shadow, fewer people in the periphery.

Honesty section

What a cenote session is not for.

We turn cenote bookings down regularly. The list below is not boilerplate — it is the reason the work that does happen looks the way it does.

i.

Not for very young children

Cenote water sits between 22–25°C year-round. Slippery limestone edges, deep central water, narrow access ladders. We photograph families with confident swimmers from roughly age ten upward; younger children we redirect to a beach session.

ii.

Not for maternity

The combination of cold groundwater, slippery rock, and the access steps rules out maternity sessions. We will not photograph a pregnant client in a cenote. The babymoon belongs at golden hour on the beach — we will book that instead.

iii.

Closed in rainy season

Roughly June through October. Storm runoff carries silt into the water table; clarity drops and the cenote register collapses. No edit pass restores it. We close the calendar to cenote work in those months and reopen in November.

iv.

Swimwear or wet wardrobe only

No leather, no beaded gowns, no shoes that mark the rock. Light fabrics that carry weight underwater — silk, chiffon, tulle, a second wedding dress. The wardrobe guide we send the week prior is specific.

v.

Cenote rules govern flash

Most cenotes permit off-camera flash; some explicitly do not. We confirm with the cenote operator the day before and adjust. The session is built so the natural light shaft does the work either way.

FAQ

Cenote-specific questions.

Can we go underwater?+

Yes, with the right cenote and the right gear. We bring underwater housing on request and choose a cenote with an entry that works for your comfort with submersion. Many of the strongest frames are half-and-half — the surface line cuts the portrait and you do not need to fully submerge to get a cinematic image.

What about non-swimmers?+

Many cenote sessions stay above water on platforms, ledges, and rock edges. Cenote Gran has a wooden platform that descends into the water; Cenote Calavera has a stone rim. We design the framing around your comfort. A life vest is provided whenever the session moves into deeper water.

When is the best time of year?+

November through April. The dry season delivers the consistent water clarity and the bright overhead sun the cenote light depends on. We close the calendar to cenote work in the rainy months because suspended particulate from runoff degrades the image, and no amount of post-production restores it.

What does it cost?+

Investment upon request. Cenote sessions are quoted per session based on which cenote, time on location, and whether underwater housing is required. Cenote entrance fees are pass-through (approximately 300 MXN at Cenote Gran, similar at Dos Ojos and Calavera). Send your travel dates and we will return a full proposal within a day.
