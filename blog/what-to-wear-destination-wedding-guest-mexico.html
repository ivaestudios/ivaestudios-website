What to Wear to a Beach Destination Wedding (Guest Guide)

By Vianey Díaz · April 21, 2026 · 8 min read

The invitation arrives, the flights are booked, and then comes the small panic that hits every guest of a destination wedding: the dress code says beach formal or tropical chic, and nobody is quite sure what that means. We photograph weddings up and down the Riviera Maya every season, which means we see what these dress codes look like in real life: who glows in the golden-hour light and who spends the ceremony fighting a stiletto sinking into the sand. This is the honest, share-it-on-your-wedding-website guide we wish every guest had before they packed.

First, Decode the Dress Code

The two phrases couples use most for a Mexican beach wedding are "beach formal" and "tropical chic," and they are not the same thing. Beach formal sits a notch below black tie. For women it means a floor-length or midi gown in a flowing fabric; for men, a linen or lightweight wool suit, usually no tie, often in a lighter color than you would wear to a city wedding. Tropical chic is a step more relaxed and a step more colorful: think a printed maxi dress or a jumpsuit, and for men a linen shirt with tailored trousers, sometimes no jacket at all. When in doubt, ask the couple what they are wearing, because their formality sets yours.

The one rule that applies to both: the venue is the beach, but the ceremony is still a wedding. We have never once heard a couple wish their guests had dressed down further. If you are deciding between two outfits, the slightly more polished one is almost always right.

The avoidable mistake

The single biggest miss we see is guests treating "beach" as a license for resort-pool casual: flip-flops, a sundress you would wear to brunch, board shorts. A destination wedding is a formal event that happens to have sand under it. Dress for the wedding, not the vacation around it.

Choose Fabrics That Survive the Heat

Here is what no one tells you until you are standing through a 5 p.m. ceremony in March: Cancun, Tulum and the Riviera Maya run hot and humid, often in the high 80s Fahrenheit with thick coastal air. Fabric is the difference between looking effortless and looking like you ran for a flight. Reach for natural, breathable weaves: linen, cotton, lightweight silk, chiffon, and crepe. They move in the sea breeze, which photographs beautifully, and they let your skin breathe.

Avoid the heat traps: polyester, heavy satin, anything lined or structured, and dark, dense fabrics that absorb the sun. A linen suit will wrinkle, and that is fine; a wrinkled linen jacket looks intentional, while a sweat-soaked polyester one does not. For color choices that flatter against turquoise water and white sand, our team's notes in our luxury weddings work hold for guests too: soft, light tones read as luminous in the golden-hour light most couples plan their ceremony around.

"A destination wedding is a formal event that simply happens to have sand under it."

Solve the Footwear Problem

Sand and stilettos are sworn enemies. A thin heel sinks on the first step, and you spend the ceremony off-balance and the reception barefoot. The fix is not to give up on looking dressed; it is to choose the right shoe. For women, wedge sandals, block heels, embellished flats, or elegant strappy sandals all stay on top of the sand. Many ceremonies on the beach are barefoot or barefoot-optional, so a beautiful pedicure quietly does a lot of work. For men, leather loafers or suede drivers beat dress shoes that fill with sand.

A practical move: if the ceremony is on sand and the reception is on a terrace or ballroom, pack two pairs. Walk in with flats for the beach portion, then change into your heels once you are on a hard floor. Most resort weddings here, whether at a property like Le Blanc in Cancun or a garden venue in the Riviera Maya, move guests from sand to a paved reception space, so a quick shoe swap is easy and nobody will notice.

Dress for the Weather and the Light

Two practical realities shape what you should pack. The first is sun and humidity: bring a hat or a pair of sunglasses for the cocktail hour, and consider a fabric that hides perspiration rather than highlighting it. The second is the after-dark shift. Beach ceremonies in Cancun and the Riviera Maya are almost always timed for the hour before sunset, which means stunning light during the vows and a real temperature drop once the sun is gone and the sea breeze picks up. A light wrap, a pashmina, or a linen blazer is worth the suitcase space, especially for receptions that run late on an open-air terrace.

Rain is the other variable. The wet season runs roughly June through October, and a tropical shower can arrive fast and pass in twenty minutes. It rarely cancels anything, but a small folding umbrella and a backup that handles a little humidity in your hair are smart. If your wedding is in Tulum or Los Cabos, the same logic applies, though Cabo runs drier and a touch breezier on the Pacific side.

Mind the Quiet Etiquette

A few unwritten rules keep you on the right side of the day. Unless the couple has explicitly invited an all-white dress code, leave white, ivory, cream and very pale blush to the wedding party; in photos, those tones read as "bride," and you do not want to be the guest who blends into her gown. Skip anything sheer or beach-sheer that looks like a cover-up, and save the swimwear for the resort.

If the couple has shared a color palette or a hashtag-worthy theme, lean into it gently rather than matching it exactly. And read the full invitation: a multi-day destination celebration often has a welcome dinner, a beach day, and the ceremony, each with its own dress note. We cover the rhythm of these events in our guide to planning a luxury destination wedding, and the short version for guests is to pack one outfit per event and not assume they are interchangeable.

When You Want to Look Your Best on Camera

Because we are usually the ones photographing these weddings, here is our last piece of advice: dress for how you will be remembered, not just for the temperature. The guests who look timeless years later are the ones in clean, breathable, well-fitted pieces in colors that suit the light, not the loudest print in the room. If you are the couple reading this and wondering how to give your guests clear guidance, we are always happy to help you word a dress code that actually works on the beach, and to make sure the photography reflects how thoughtfully everyone showed up.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does "beach formal" actually mean for a guest?

It sits just below black tie. For women, a floor-length or midi gown in a flowing fabric like chiffon or silk; for men, a linen or lightweight suit, usually without a tie and often in a lighter color than a city wedding. It is dressy, just adapted for sand and heat.

Can I wear heels to a beach wedding ceremony?

Thin stilettos sink into sand, so they are not ideal for the ceremony itself. Choose wedges, block heels, or elegant flats for the beach portion, then pack a second pair of heels to change into once you reach a paved or terrace reception.

Is it okay to wear white to a destination beach wedding?

No, unless the couple has specifically requested an all-white dress code. White, ivory, cream and very pale blush read as bridal in photos. Save those tones for the wedding party and choose a color instead.

How do I stay cool in the Cancun heat without looking underdressed?

Pick natural, breathable fabrics like linen, cotton, chiffon and crepe in lighter colors that reflect the sun. They keep you cool and move beautifully in the sea breeze. Bring a light wrap or linen blazer for the temperature drop after sunset.
