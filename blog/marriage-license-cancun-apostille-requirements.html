How to Get a Marriage License in Cancun: Apostille & Paperwork

Before we ever talk about light or a venue, the studio gets one question from couples flying in from the States, Canada or Europe: are we actually married when we leave Cancun, or is this just a pretty ceremony on a beach? It is the right thing to ask first, because the paperwork answer changes your whole timeline. A legally binding marriage in Mexico is a civil act handled by the government, not by your resort or your planner, and it runs on apostilles, certified translations and a short residency window that has to be lined up months before you board the plane. We are photographers, not attorneys, so treat this as the honest field guide we give our own couples, then confirm every detail with your venue's legal coordinator and the local registry.

The First Decision: Legal or Symbolic

There are two completely different ways to "get married" in Cancun, and most international couples we photograph choose the simpler one without realizing it. A legal (civil) marriage is registered with the Quintana Roo Registro Civil, the state civil registry, and it is the version that produces a Mexican marriage certificate recognized back home. A symbolic ceremony is the beach vows, the officiant, the aisle and the rings, with no government filing at all. It looks identical in the photographs, but it carries no legal weight.

Here is the honest truth the studio sees on the ground: a large share of American and European couples handle the legal signing quietly at a courthouse at home, then fly to Mexico for a symbolic ceremony that is the real celebration. It removes the translation and apostille pressure from a foreign country and lets the Cancun day be purely about the experience. If you want the marriage itself to happen on Mexican soil, that is absolutely possible too, and the rest of this guide walks you through exactly what that takes. Either path photographs beautifully across a luxury wedding in the region.

Apostille and Certified Translation: The Two Non-Negotiables

If you marry legally in Quintana Roo, every foreign document you submit has to clear two hurdles before the registry will accept it. The first is the apostille. Mexico and most Western countries belong to the Hague Apostille Convention, so instead of full embassy legalization you get a single apostille certificate attached to your civil documents. In the United States this is issued by your state's Secretary of State (the state that issued the birth certificate, not where you live now). In Canada it is now an apostille as well since Canada joined the Convention in early 2024. In the United Kingdom it is the FCDO, and across the EU it is the relevant national authority. Your core document is your long-form birth certificate; if either of you is divorced or widowed, the apostilled divorce decree or the prior spouse's death certificate usually comes into play too.

The second hurdle is the certified translation. Every apostilled English or French document, plus the apostille itself, must be translated into Spanish by a perito traductor, an official translator authorized by the Quintana Roo courts. A translation you ordered at home almost never qualifies, which is why most couples have their venue's legal coordinator arrange the local perito. Build in real time here: ordering a long-form certificate, getting it apostilled by mail, and then having it translated locally can easily span two to three months end to end.

Start the apostille first

The single most common timeline mistake we see is couples booking the date, the resort and the photographer, then starting the apostille four weeks out. State offices and mail-order apostille services run on their own clock, and a wrong-format birth certificate sends you back to square one. Order long-form birth certificates and begin the apostille the moment you have a wedding month in mind, ideally three to four months ahead.

Passports, Witnesses and the Residency Window

Beyond the apostilled paperwork, Quintana Roo asks for a short list of in-person essentials. You both need valid passports and the tourist entry record (the FMM / migration stamp you receive on arrival). You need four witnesses over the age of eighteen with valid government ID; if you are not bringing your own, planners can often supply them, but confirm in advance because a missing witness can stall the signing. There is also a mandatory pre-marital blood test drawn at a local Cancun lab within a few days of the ceremony, screening for items the state requires, and the results have to be in hand before the civil judge will officiate.

Then there is the part that surprises people most: a residency window. Mexico generally expects couples to be in-country for a few business days before a civil ceremony, partly so the local blood test and the judge's appointment can be scheduled. In practice the studio plans for couples to land at least three to four business days before a legal Cancun wedding, with weekends and Mexican holidays not counting. A symbolic ceremony has none of this, which is the other reason it stays so popular with destination couples.

"The marriage is a government act, not a resort amenity. Plan the paperwork with the same care you plan the sunset, because only one of them can be fixed on the day."

Who Actually Files It (and Where the Studio Fits)

You do not navigate the Registro Civil alone, and you should not try to. Every serious resort and wedding planner in Cancun and the Riviera Maya has a legal coordinator whose entire job is shepherding foreign documents through the local civil registry, booking the judge, arranging the perito translator and scheduling the blood test. When you interview venues, this is a question worth asking directly: do they handle legal civil ceremonies in-house, and what is their document deadline? Some properties only facilitate symbolic ceremonies and quietly expect you to arrive already married. There is nothing wrong with that, but you want to know before you assume.

The studio's role sits right alongside theirs. Once your legal path is settled, we build the photography timeline around the ceremony the registry will actually allow, and around the honest Cancun light rather than the brochure. If you are weaving the legal signing and the symbolic celebration into a single trip, we map both so neither feels rushed. You can see how that full arc comes together on our destination wedding photography page, and our wedding-day timeline guide in the Journal shows how the hours fit together once the paperwork is behind you.

Where the Paperwork Lands in Your Budget

The legal route carries real, if modest, costs that are easy to forget when you are pricing flowers and an open bar: state-by-state apostille fees, expedited mail for the certificates, the local certified translation per document, the blood-test fee, and the judge's appointment. None of it is large on its own, but it is paperwork money that the symbolic route skips entirely. For couples weighing the trade-off, we lay out the broader numbers in our Mexico destination wedding cost breakdown and the local view in Cancun wedding costs for 2026.

Our standing advice to the couples we work with: decide legal-versus-symbolic before you book anything else, because that one choice dictates your arrival date, your three-to-four-month document runway, and how relaxed your actual wedding morning will be. Most luxury couples we photograph land on a quiet legal signing at home and a full-hearted symbolic ceremony in Cancun, and they never regret keeping the foreign paperwork off the celebration day.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a wedding ceremony in Cancun legally binding in the US or Canada?

Only if you complete a civil (legal) marriage with the Quintana Roo Registro Civil, which then issues a Mexican marriage certificate recognized at home. A symbolic beach ceremony, even with vows and an officiant, carries no legal weight. Many couples sign legally at home and hold a symbolic ceremony in Cancun.

What documents do I need to legally marry in Cancun?

At minimum: valid passports, your tourist entry record, apostilled long-form birth certificates with certified Spanish translations, four witnesses with ID, and a local pre-marital blood test. Divorced or widowed couples also need an apostilled divorce decree or death certificate. Your venue's legal coordinator confirms the exact current list.

How early should I start the apostille process?

Three to four months before the wedding is the safe window. Ordering long-form birth certificates, getting them apostilled by your state or national authority, and then having them translated locally by an authorized perito traductor can easily take two to three months combined.

How many days before the wedding do we have to arrive in Cancun?

For a legal civil ceremony, plan to land at least three to four business days early so the blood test and the civil judge's appointment can be scheduled. Weekends and Mexican holidays generally do not count. A symbolic ceremony has no residency requirement at all.
