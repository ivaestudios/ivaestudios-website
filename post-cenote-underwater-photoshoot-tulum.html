A cenote portrait can be the strongest single frame of a Riviera Maya trip, or it can be a forgettable image of an extraordinary place. The difference is rarely the camera. It is timing, water clarity, and a refusal to overwork the scene. This is the working brief our team uses when we plan an underwater session in Tulum — the cenotes, the windows, the equipment, and the conditions under which we say yes.

Why a cenote, and why with restraint

The Yucatán cenotes are among the most photogenic interiors on the continent — limestone galleries opened to the sky, fresh water filtered through stone, ancient stalactites holding still light. They are also among the easiest places to overshoot. The temptation is to chase every angle: the beam, the silhouette, the half-and-half, the deep dive, the surface mirror. Most of those frames look the same after the third one.

Our editorial position is the opposite. One cenote, one window, two or three considered frames. The cenote does the heavy work; we hold back. The result is a small set of images that read as portraits in a place rather than tourism inside a cave.

This entry is the long-form companion to our service page on cenote photography. The service page is the brief; this is the reasoning behind it.

Three cenotes we work in — and why

There are hundreds of cenotes within an hour of Tulum. We work in three. The criteria are simple: water clarity that holds in the dry season, an opening geometry that sends usable light into the frame, and access we can rely on without negotiating with a tourist crowd. Below are the three.

Cenote Gran

Four kilometres from Tulum centre, semi-open, with a wooden platform that descends gently into the water. Gran is the cenote we recommend first. It rewards both above-water portraits at the rim and fully submerged frames among the stalactites without forcing the subject to choose. Its real signature is the columnar sunbeam that arrives between roughly 11:00 and 13:00 in the dry season — described in detail below. Entrance is a pass-through cost (about 300 MXN). We arrive before the gate opens, set the housing, and leave before the day-trip buses reach the platform.

Cenote Dos Ojos

Twenty-two kilometres north on the 307. Two connected chambers, each accessed through a circular opening, with the stronger photographic light in the first chamber. Visibility on a clean dry-season day exceeds thirty metres. Dos Ojos is the option we choose when the work calls for scale — the cathedral feel, the long stalactite ceiling, the deeper blue. It rewards experienced subjects who are comfortable submerging for short, deliberate sequences. Entry roughly 400 MXN per person, opens at 08:00.

Cenote Calavera

Skull cenote — named for the three openings in its limestone cap that resemble a skull from above. Smaller than Gran, more intimate, with a stone rim the body holds onto without effort. Calavera is our quiet alternative when we want the moodier register of the four light registers in our brand work: the marina-blue shadow, the moisture on stone, an editorial that earns its restraint. Less crowded than the headline cenotes; reachable in fifteen minutes from the Tulum hotel zone. Entrance approximately 250 MXN.

The 11–1 light window at Gran

Gran's reputation rests on a single physical phenomenon: when the sun is high enough overhead, a column of light enters the cavern opening at an angle steep enough to penetrate the water column. The frame is the column, lit, with a subject in or beside it.

The window is narrow and predictable. Between approximately 11:00 and 13:00 from late November through April, the geometry holds. Outside that window the cenote remains beautiful in ambient soft light — but the beam frames, the ones the cenote is known for, exist only inside that two-hour band. Around the December solstice the window tightens to roughly 11:30–12:30. We confirm exact timing per date when we send the call sheet.

Practical implication: a Gran session is a midday session. We arrive on the platform at 10:30, configure the housing in the soft pre-window light, and start working frames the moment the column resolves. We stop at 13:00 even if we could keep shooting; the column has gone, and another twenty minutes of half-light dilutes the gallery rather than extending it.

Water clarity by season

The other variable is the water itself. Cenote water is filtered groundwater, which is why it can hold the visibility it does. It is also why it carries the season directly.

Dry season — November to April

This is when we book cenote sessions. Visibility holds at twenty to thirty metres, the column light at Gran behaves predictably, and water temperature stays close to 25°C. Within this window, January and February are the most reliable months; humidity is lowest and the pre-window ambient light is at its most controlled.

Rainy season — May to October

We do not work cenotes in the rainy months. Surface runoff carries suspended particulate — tannins from leaf litter, fine clay from the jungle floor — into the cenote system. Visibility drops by two-thirds within hours of a serious rain event, and the recovery time can be days. The clarity that defines a cenote frame is gone, and editing does not recover it. When clients ask for a June or July cenote shoot, we redirect to a beach session at Riviera Maya resorts where the variable is light, not visibility — and light is something we can plan.

Shoulder weeks

Late October and early November sit between the two regimes. We open cenote bookings on a case-by-case basis once the rains have visibly stopped and the water has cleared. We will reschedule a shoulder-week cenote session at no charge if visibility on the morning of the shoot is not where it should be.

Underwater housing and light controls

Equipment is the part of the brief most often oversold. Our position is that underwater gear is necessary for a cenote session and not interesting beyond that fact. We use a Nauticam-class housing rated to 60 metres, a wide dome port that controls refraction at the surface line, and a pair of remote-triggered underwater strobes — all of which most working underwater photographers in this region carry.

The decision that matters is when to use the strobes and when not to. At Gran, in the column window, we shoot ambient. The cenote provides the light; adding strobes flattens the contrast that is the entire point of the frame. At Dos Ojos in the deeper chamber, we use a single back-strobe at low power to define the subject without lifting the cavern. At Calavera, where the registers we want are moody and earth-toned, ambient light is again the right choice.

What does change session to session is the diopter and lens combination, the dome port size for the framing we have planned, and the strobe placement when we do use them. The call sheet specifies all three before the day, so nothing is improvised on the platform.

Wardrobe and direction below the surface

The wardrobe rule is the inverse of beach work: weight wins. A garment that fans on a beach floats inertly in fresh water; one engineered to fall in air will billow under the surface in a way that reads on camera. Tulle, silk chiffon, light organza, and unhemmed cotton in lengths that exceed the body all behave correctly. We send a wardrobe note with the booking confirmation that names two specific dress shapes we have shot in each cenote and how each behaves on entry.

Colour is more constrained than people expect. White and ivory carry the available light through the column and read against the dark water. Soft blush, pale champagne, and the warm sand register from our brand palette also photograph cleanly. Saturated jewel tones absorb light below the surface and look murkier on camera than they did on the rim.

Direction underwater is a small, repeated set: enter the water from the platform, settle, hold position, breathe out slowly to descend. The shutter fires in a short burst; the subject surfaces; we review the frame and run again. There is no extended breath holding and no posing. The work is structural, not performative.

Honest restrictions

A cenote session is not for everyone, and we say so before booking rather than after.

  • Not for very young children. Cold groundwater, low cavern temperature, and the breath-control demands of the underwater register make this an editorial format designed for couples and adventurous adult families. Families with small children are better served by a sunset session on the beach, where the energy of children is the picture and not a logistical liability.
  • Not in the rainy season. We hold the line on May–October closures. The frames that make a cenote session worth booking do not exist when visibility collapses.
  • Not in extended jewellery or heavy make-up. Loose stones are lost in the water. Standard make-up will not survive submersion. We send a brief on both before the session.
  • Not as a stand-alone trip. The cenote belongs inside a longer Riviera Maya stay. We schedule the cenote session as one chapter of a wider plan that usually includes a beach session at the resort and, where appropriate, a portrait at the ruins or at the resort's signature location.

How we book a cenote session

Booking starts with a short note: travel dates, group size, the resort, and any specific cenote preference. We answer within twenty-four hours with the dates that work, the recommended cenote for those dates, and the wardrobe note. A retainer holds the calendar slot; the balance settles a week before the session.

Most cenote sessions sit inside a longer engagement. If you are also planning beach coverage, an editorial portrait of a maternity stage, or a wedding moment in the same trip, send all of it in the first message and we will design the week as a single sequence rather than three independent bookings. The detail of how we work region-wide is on the Riviera Maya page; the cenote-specific brief is on /cenote-photography.

Frequent questions

When does the light window open at Cenote Gran?
Approximately 11:00 to 13:00 from late November through April. Outside that window the cenote remains beautiful in soft, ambient light, but the dramatic beam frames are a midday phenomenon. We schedule arrival 30 minutes before the window opens and confirm exact timing per date on the call sheet.
Why does IVAE Studios refuse cenote work in the rainy season?
From May through October, runoff carries suspended particulate from the jungle into the cenote system. Visibility drops, the iconic clarity is gone, and no amount of post-production restores it. We close the cenote calendar in those months and redirect those sessions to the beach. Cenote bookings open again in November.
Can children be part of a cenote underwater session?
Cenote underwater sessions are not appropriate for very young children. Cold groundwater, low ambient temperature, and the demands of breath holding make this an editorial format designed for couples and adventurous adult families. We are happy to design alternative sessions on the beach or above-water at the cenote rim for families with small children.
What underwater equipment does IVAE Studios use?
A Nauticam-class housing rated to 60 metres, a wide dome port to control refraction at the surface line, and a pair of remote-triggered underwater strobes calibrated to the cenote rather than a fixed preset. Equipment is briefed on the call sheet, not as a sales line.
Do I need to know how to swim?
Comfort with submersion matters more than swimming technique. Many of the strongest frames are half-and-half, where the surface line cuts the portrait and you do not need to fully submerge. Cenote Gran has a wooden platform; Cenote Calavera has a stone rim. We design framing around your comfort and provide a flotation aid whenever the session moves into deeper water.
What does the session cost?
Investment upon request. Cenote sessions are quoted per session based on which cenote, time on location, and whether we are pairing the cenote with beach or resort coverage in the same trip. Cenote entrance fees are pass-through (approximately 250 to 400 MXN depending on the cenote). Send your travel dates for a custom proposal.
