Journal · Weddings

Vow renewals, edited. The anniversary and the ceremony, in one quiet hour.

Five, ten, twenty-five, fifty years in. The trip is the gift; the ceremony is the punctuation. A vow renewal is the quietest assignment we shoot — and often the most direct.

April 2, 2026 · ~6 min read · By Vianey Ortega

A vow renewal is not a smaller wedding; it is a different document. Most of the couples who book one with us are travelling for the anniversary anyway — fifth, tenth, twenty-fifth, fiftieth — and the ceremony is something they decide to add once they are already on the coast. There is no procession, no guest list to negotiate, no reception. There is one hour at the right end of the afternoon, and there is the photograph that comes back home with them.

Because the renewal is symbolic rather than legal, the day is unusually free of logistics. No paperwork, no Registro Civil filing, no apostille. The couple decides where to stand, what to say, and who, if anyone, is watching. That freedom is also the part most couples underestimate the value of — until they see how much of an editorial session lives in what is not happening.

Five, ten, twenty-five, fifty

Each milestone arrives with its own posture. The five-year couples we photograph tend to bring children, sometimes very young; the renewal becomes a family portrait disguised as a ceremony. The ten-year mark is the most common booking by volume — couples who married small, or married fast, and want a frame for the version of themselves they are now. Twenty-five and fifty are quieter still; we shoot them at long resort beaches at the end of the day, often without a third person present.

What every milestone shares is restraint. The couples who book vow renewals are almost universally past the production phase of life. They do not want a bridal party, a DJ, or a string of champagne tables. They want a thirty-minute walk and a few honest minutes of words. Our job is to compose around the words.

The ceremony is the punctuation. Everything before and after is the sentence.

Where it tends to happen

Three locations carry most of the renewals we photograph. A long Riviera Maya beach at the end of the second hour before sunset — see our Riviera Maya overview for the stretches we return to. A private resort cove on the Cancún Playa Mujeres or Costa Mujeres coast, where the architecture is doing some of the work. Or, less often but reliably striking, a Los Cabos cliffside at the moment the light turns warm against the rock.

Cenotes work for renewals, but only in a particular configuration — usually a couple revisiting the location of their first elopement or destination wedding. Symmetry is the structuring idea, and most of the planning collapses to one decision: the same place, ten or twenty years later.

The session, structured

A typical renewal runs ninety minutes. The first thirty are pre-ceremony portraits, usually walking and quiet — nothing posed, the couple settling. The ceremony itself is twenty to thirty minutes, photographed at a respectful distance, no direction during it. Then forty minutes of directed portraits in the warmest portion of golden hour: the walk back, a still frame, an embrace held longer than feels normal because it is the only time you are asked to.

Most couples add a private dinner sequence — a final twenty minutes at the table, candlelight, the rings, a last frame at the door. That dinner is the part most couples remember. The vows are often whispered; the dinner is the one shared meal a year that gets photographed.

What to wear when you have done this before

The first wedding's dress is rarely the right one. Skin has changed; the body has lived a life. The renewal is the ceremony where comfort and confidence outweigh tradition. We see most couples in lightweight, simple silhouettes — silk slip, ivory chiffon, a long linen suit unstructured at the shoulder. Earth tones photograph beautifully against Caribbean light; stark white reads harsh. Our outfit brief lands in your inbox a month out, tailored to the location you booked.

If you are bringing the children

Roughly a third of vow renewals we shoot include children — usually two or three, no more. The children are not in the ceremony. They sit, briefly, at the edge of the frame; we photograph them separately, before or after, in a fifteen-minute family portrait sequence. Trying to make them part of the ceremony itself dilutes the photograph for everyone. Better to give them their own document.

For couples thinking about how the day fits with broader couples photography, our couples portraiture page shows the visual grammar that translates directly into a renewal session. Same direction, different occasion.

What the gallery looks like

Around 100 to 150 hand-culled frames, delivered in three business days. The cull is hand-done; we do not deliver every frame. We deliver the ones that read together as one editorial set. Couples often print one large frame for the wall and a small album for the dresser. The gallery is private, password-protected, and downloadable in print and web sizes.
