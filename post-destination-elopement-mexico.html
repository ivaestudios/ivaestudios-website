Journal · Weddings

Tulum cenote elopements, considered. A working brief for two.

The most directed wedding format we shoot. Two people, one location, no audience. Below is how we structure the day — what to expect, how the light behaves, and why a Tulum cenote remains the signature subgenre.

April 2, 2026 · ~6 min read · By Vianey Ortega

An elopement, the way we shoot it, is not a smaller wedding. It is a different document entirely. There is no schedule shared with fifteen vendors, no processional, no group portraits to stage between cocktail hour and dinner. There is one couple, one window of light, and a small handful of decisions that must be right.

Most of the elopements that come through the studio fall into one of three configurations. The Tulum cenote — limestone walls, freshwater shaft, the light dropping through a single opening overhead. A Riviera Maya cliff or boutique resort beach — quiet, private, scaled for two. Or a Los Cabos cove — Pacific drama, harder light, a sense of distance from the resort coast. Each one rewards a different pacing.

Why Tulum cenotes became the signature

Cenotes are uncommon enough that the location does most of the work. The room is already built — limestone, freshwater, a single shaft of overhead light that moves a few degrees over the course of an hour. There is no decoration to add, no scrim to fight, no stranger to direct around. The acoustics carry a quiet vow with no microphone.

For couples who want the day to feel intentional but not produced, a private cenote is the closest a location gets to decoration that decorates itself. We schedule the ceremony in the second hour after the cenote opens, before the light flattens, and we leave with portraits before the day's heat builds. The whole sequence runs ninety minutes from arrival.

The light is doing the choreography. We just decide when to walk in.

Beyond Tulum, the broader Riviera Maya offers ceremony options that read very differently on camera — Akumal beach openings at golden hour, Mayakoba's lagoon chapel for couples who want architecture as much as nature, and the quieter coves around Puerto Aventuras. Each is matched to a couple by light register before anything else.

The decisions, in order

An elopement is decided in five questions, and they go in this order: light window, location, officiant, attire, witnesses. Anything outside those five is logistics, not direction. Couples who try to decide attire before the light tend to end up changing it once the location is locked. The light leads.

Symbolic ceremonies handle the day for roughly four out of five couples we photograph. The legal paperwork happens at home, before or after the trip — no apostille, no blood test, no Spanish translation of the birth certificate. The ceremony in Mexico is whatever the two of you want it to be, with whichever officiant you trust to read the room. For couples who do want the legal ceremony performed in Mexico, the Registro Civil requires three working days of advance filing and four witnesses; our team coordinates that timeline with a bilingual planner.

Attire, edited for movement

Cenotes run cool — low 70s Fahrenheit at the water, even in May. Beaches run hot. Both reward fabric that moves. Silk crepe, organza, lightweight chiffon, linen for the suit. Skip anything structured enough to fight the wind; the wind is the second photographer here.

Barefoot reads as intentional in sand and around limestone. We send a one-page outfit brief once the date is locked, tailored to the chosen location and the season. The outfit guide does not chase trends — it answers what photographs well in the IVAE color register, in the light you booked.

How the session moves

A two-hour elopement covers the ceremony itself, a private first-look or post-vow walk, and roughly forty minutes of directed portraits as the light softens. Couples often book a third hour for a private dinner sequence — table detail, candlelight, a final frame at the door of the suite. That third hour is the one most couples remember most vividly a year out.

For a sense of how the same couples-photography vocabulary plays out across non-elopement formats, see our couples portraiture page. The grammar is identical. Only the document changes.

What the gallery looks like

Between 120 and 200 hand-culled frames, delivered inside three business days. Color is unified across the set so the gallery reads as a single editorial story rather than a collection of moments. No one warm, one flat. The cull is done by hand, never automated; the strongest frames are the ones we hand back.
