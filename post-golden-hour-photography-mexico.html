Golden hour in Mexico — the 70-minute window.

Golden hour is not a romance. It is a 70-minute window where the sun sits low enough to flatter every face and every fabric. This is the philosophy behind every session at IVAE Studios — and how the three coastlines we work shape it differently.

April 2, 2026 · ~7 min read · By Vianey Ortega

Most photographers describe golden hour as a feeling. We describe it as a schedule. The thirty-five minutes before sunset and the thirty-five minutes after sunrise are the only windows where the sun sits low enough to wrap a face in warm, directional light without forcing the eyes to squint or the shadows to harden. Every IVAE session is built around that physics — the rest of the day is, with rare exceptions, a different photograph altogether.

This is the article we hand clients when they ask why the call sheet says 5:42 PM and not 4:00 PM. It is also the reason a Cancún photograph at noon and a Cancún photograph at sunset look like two entirely different countries.

The physics, briefly

When the sun sits within roughly six degrees of the horizon, its light travels through more atmosphere before reaching the subject. The atmosphere scatters short blue wavelengths and lets warm reds, oranges and ambers come through — that is the colour shift the eye reads as golden. The same low angle produces three other effects that matter just as much: long shadows that sculpt cheekbones, soft directional fall-off across white linen and skin, and an exposure low enough that a person can look toward the light without flinching. Four variables, all aligned, for about thirty-five minutes per side of the day.

This is why the IVAE process locks the session window in writing before anything else. The outfit guide, the location walk-through, the wind read — all of those are built around a fixed start time. The light decides the schedule, not the calendar.

The light decides, not the photographer.

Three coastlines, three answers

Mexico's three IVAE regions face different oceans and behave differently inside the same hour. Knowing which way your resort faces is the difference between a front-lit golden frame and a flat backlit silhouette where you wanted a portrait.

Cancún — east-facing, sunrise is the hero

The entire Hotel Zone faces the Caribbean to the east. The sun rises directly out of the turquoise water and front-lights the beach for the first thirty-five minutes of the day. The Atlantic blue register becomes editorial in that window. Sunset still works at the lagoon side and inside resort architecture, but for front-lit warmth on the open beach, sunrise is the Cancún advantage. We schedule sunrise for couples who want privacy, and sunset for the wide pool decks and west-facing terraces.

Riviera Maya — the soft south-southeast curve

The coastline curves between Playa del Carmen, Akumal and Tulum, which gives most beaches a south-southeast orientation. That geometry is unusually generous: depending on the resort and the time of year, either end of the day can deliver direct, warm light. We pair sunrise sessions with the colder pastel skies that the jungle line produces and reserve sunset for the warmer amber tones that bounce off the limestone shelf. Cenote sessions sit outside golden hour entirely — the dramatic shaft of midday light through the cenote opening is its own discipline.

Los Cabos — Pacific west, sunset is the headline

Los Cabos faces the Pacific. The sun drops directly into the ocean for the final thirty minutes of every day, and the colour temperature pushes toward copper. El Arco silhouettes against magenta sky. The light is more saturated than on the Caribbean side and the window feels longer because of how slowly the sun moves at that latitude. For sheer sunset drama, no Mexican coastline competes.

Sunrise, sunset, and the practical difference

The colour temperature of sunrise and sunset are within a few hundred Kelvin of each other — the eye reads them as nearly identical. The differences are practical. Sunrise gives you empty beaches and cooler air, but it requires a 4:30 AM call time for hair and wardrobe. Sunset is calmer to schedule, but the shoreline is busier and the body language is end-of-day rather than start-of-day. For our luxury family sessions with young children, sunset is almost always the more reliable answer. For couples who want total privacy on the sand, sunrise wins. For weddings and elopements, the ceremony time decides.

Blue hour — the twenty minutes after the sun is gone

Once the sun drops below the horizon, the sky transitions through pink and magenta into deep blue for about twenty minutes. There are no shadows. The exposure shifts every minute. Silhouettes become editorial and graphic against vivid sky — some of the strongest frames of every session live here. We schedule blue hour as a deliberate final chapter, not an accident.

What the call sheet looks like

Twenty-four hours before the session, every IVAE client receives a one-page call sheet. It includes the exact local sunset (or sunrise) time, the golden hour window calculated for that date and resort, a fifteen-minute arrival buffer, and a weather contingency. Hair and makeup are scheduled to finish forty-five minutes before arrival. Sunscreen goes on at least two hours earlier so it absorbs without leaving a sheen on skin. Wardrobe is steamed the night before. The session itself is sixty to ninety minutes — long enough to settle, short enough to stay inside the light.

The clients who produce the strongest galleries are not the ones with perfect hair or designer outfits. They are the ones who arrive on time, hand the schedule to us, and let the light do its work.
