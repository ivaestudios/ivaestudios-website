A destination wedding, planned from abroad — considered.

Editorial restraint where traditional wedding coverage over-shoots. A working brief for couples flying into the Riviera Maya, Cancún, or Los Cabos with three days, one planner, and a single chance to get the day right.

April 21, 2026 · ~7 min read · By Vianey Ortega

A destination wedding is not, in the end, a logistics problem. It is an editorial decision repeated forty times in a row — what to include, what to leave out, when to be present, when to stand back. The couples who travel best to Mexico for their wedding are not the ones with the longest spreadsheet. They are the ones who decided, before the first flight was booked, what kind of pictures they wanted to live with.

What follows is the working brief we share with international clients planning their year ahead with us — across Riviera Maya, Cancún, and Los Cabos. It is the order in which we would think about your wedding if we were planning it from the inside.

Choose the coastline, not the resort.

The three regions we shoot are not interchangeable. They photograph differently, plan differently, and reward different temperaments.

Riviera Maya — Tulum to Puerto Morelos — is the editorial coast: jungle architecture, low-density beaches, cenotes within driving distance, and resorts that lean restrained rather than maximal. It is our most-shot region, and the one where you have the widest range of looks inside a single day. It rewards couples who want a wedding that does not look like everyone else's. Cancún — particularly Costa Mujeres and Punta Nizuc — is the most operationally polished coast: the resorts are larger, the planners more practiced, and the airport is twenty minutes away. It rewards couples bringing more guests, more vendors, more moving parts. Los Cabos photographs unlike either: desert meets Pacific, dry light, dramatic rock formations, golden hour that tilts copper instead of pink. It rewards couples who want their wedding to feel cinematic rather than tropical.

We are happy to talk through which coast suits the wedding you have in mind. The honest answer is rarely the one you find on a venue brochure.

Travel logistics, ranked by the photographs.

A useful inversion: stop optimizing your wedding for guest convenience and start optimizing for the four hours that matter on camera. Three rules we share with every couple before they book a flight.

  1. Arrive forty-eight hours before the ceremony, minimum. Dehydration shows in skin tone for a full day after a long flight. Mexico is unforgiving with that. Forty-eight hours buys you a clean face on camera and a planning call in person.
  2. Build the timeline backward from sunset, not forward from arrival. Your ceremony hour is set by the light at your venue on your specific date — sixty minutes before sunset, plus or minus ten depending on the season. Every other guest moment slots around that fixed point. Your planner will thank you.
  3. Treat the welcome dinner as half a session. The night before, in resort architecture, with quiet light and rested faces, produces some of the strongest editorial frames of the entire trip. Couples often regret not booking it; never the reverse.

Your planner is not optional.

A traditional wedding photographer can work in a vacuum. A destination photographer cannot. The planner — yours, or one your resort assigns — is the person who decides whether the chairs are placed where the photographer asked, whether the bouquets reach the bride before the first look, whether the second-line entrance happens on the beat the music was timed to. We do not take a destination wedding without a named planner on the chain. The reason is mathematical: distance multiplies every coordination failure by three.

We work directly with the wedding planners at most major luxury resort partners across the three regions, and we are happy to recommend independents we have shot alongside many times. The right planner is the difference between a day you describe to friends and a day you ask us to print.

A destination wedding rewards the couple who decides what to leave out — and the photographer who agrees with them.

Editorial restraint, not maximal coverage.

Most wedding galleries you have seen carry a thousand images. We do not deliver galleries that size. The shape of an IVAE wedding is closer to a magazine: edited tightly, sequenced deliberately, weighted toward the frames that survive the year — not the year-one-only frames a content shooter would over-include.

What this looks like in practice: fewer ceremony reverse-angles, more arrival; fewer cake-cutting permutations, more first-look tension; fewer guest reactions during speeches, more architecture in the empty venue at the hour the room finally exhales. The result is a gallery you can sit through on a Sunday morning two years from now without skipping pages. A traditional wedding album is exhausting. An editorial one is not.

A few of our recent destination weddings live in the portfolio. The shape there is the shape we work to — across all three regions.

The session.

A destination wedding day at IVAE Studios is one team across photo and motion, a single planning call before you fly in, a printed timeline in your hand on arrival, and galleries delivered within three business days of the wedding — the speed matters when half your guests left the country the morning after. Communication is bilingual end-to-end with you, your planner, and the resort.

We take a small number of weddings each year, in Riviera Maya, Cancún, and Los Cabos. If yours is on the calendar, send us your travel dates and a few words on the resort. We will write back with availability and the structure we would propose.
