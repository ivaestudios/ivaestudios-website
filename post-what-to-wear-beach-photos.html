Journal · Outfit

What to wear for beach photos — the IVAE register.

A short colour palette and three fabrics that hold their shape in the wind. This is the long-form companion to our Outfit Guide — written so you arrive at the beach without a single decision left to make.

April 2, 2026 · ~7 min read · By Vianey Ortega

The reason resort photos look chaotic is that nothing was decided about wardrobe before the trip began. Five suitcases, five colour stories, five different fabrics catching the wind in five different ways. The frame goes wide and the eye has nowhere to land. Editorial does the opposite. Editorial decides on a register, holds it across every figure, and lets the coastline do the rest.

This piece is the long-form companion to the Outfit Guide we send every booked client. The guide is one page, tactical, ready to print. This is the why. Read it once before you start packing.

The IVAE colour register

Our four working colours are the same ones the studio is built on: Resort White, Atlantic Navy, Soft Slate and Warm Sand. They are not arbitrary. Each one earns its place in beach light because of what it does to skin and what it does to the surrounding palette.

Resort White — the protagonist

Resort White is a warm off-white — closer to oyster than to chalk. It picks up the colour temperature of the hour: pink at sunrise, amber at sunset, neutral in open shade. It carries the most light back into the face, which is why we put the lead figure (the bride, the mother, the silhouette in front) in white more often than in any other tone. Pure surgical white reads cold under tropical sun and tends to spike highlights; an off-white linen or silk holds its detail.

Atlantic Navy — the anchor

Deep navy reads almost black against turquoise water and grounds the frame. We use it sparingly — one figure, maybe a pair of trousers, a swim shorts — because two navies side by side compete with the ocean instead of partnering with it. A navy linen shirt over a Resort White dress is the most reliable couples combination we shoot.

Soft Slate — the bridge

A muted blue-gray that sits between Resort White and Atlantic Navy. It is the colour for the second adult in a family frame, or the third bridesmaid, or the figure who needs to recede slightly so the lead reads first. Slate is forgiving on most skin tones and never fights for attention.

Warm Sand — the warmth

Sand, oat, camel, dune. Earth tones that come from the same family as the beach itself. Warm Sand is the answer for anyone who feels uncomfortable in head-to-toe white — it reads softer, gives the gallery a documentary warmth, and pairs cleanly with both navy and white. We avoid pure beige (it can read flat) and lean toward sand with a hint of pink or olive in it.

Editorial Gold appears in our brand work as a rare accent. On wardrobe, treat it the same way: a thin gold chain, a wedding band, the ornament on a sandal. Never gold as a dominant garment colour — it spikes under sun and dates the photograph.

The register is short on purpose. Three figures, two colours, one frame.

Three fabrics that move in the wind

The Caribbean and the Pacific are not still environments. The breeze on a Riviera Maya beach in February runs ten to fifteen knots most evenings; in Cabo it can pick up to twenty in the late afternoon. Wardrobe is not just colour — it is the way fabric reacts to that wind. Three materials do this well. Most others do not.

Linen

Linen is the workhorse of beach photography. It moves with the wind in long, slow folds, never in stiff geometric crinkles. It absorbs sweat without showing it. It crops cleanly because the weave is loose enough to read texture even at distance. We recommend a slightly oversized linen (a half-size up from your usual fit) so the silhouette has somewhere to flow rather than stretch flat against the body.

Cotton

Soft cotton works for children, for shirting, and for any garment that needs to keep its shape when the wearer is sitting in the sand. Avoid stiff cotton (poplin, oxford) on the beach — it creases sharply and reads as unironed travel clothing. Look for cotton with a soft hand, ideally pre-washed or stone-finished.

Silk and silk blends

Silk is the third fabric, used carefully. A silk slip dress at sunset, a silk camisole under a linen shirt, a silk scarf as a hair accessory — silk catches the warm light of golden hour and holds it. The caution: silk shows water-spray immediately. If we are shooting close to the surf line, we move silk to the early portion of the session and switch to linen for the wave-edge frames.

What we do not use: synthetics that read plastic in tropical light (polyester, technical fabrics), denim heavier than a chambray (it overheats and crops poorly), and any fabric that requires shapewear underneath. The light is too good to fight a garment that fights you back.

What to avoid

The shortest section of the brief, and the most important. Three things never come to a beach session.

  • Logos and prominent branding. Visible logos — chest letters, monogram patterns, brand text on a tote — date a photograph the moment it appears. They become the first thing the eye sees, and they pull the frame from editorial to advertisement.
  • Neon and saturated synthetics. Hot pink, fluorescent yellow, electric coral. Tropical sun is already saturated; a neon garment becomes the only thing the camera sees and the rest of the family disappears into the background.
  • Anything tight at the waist for maternity sessions. A fitted waistband under the bump cuts the silhouette in half and works against the line we are trying to draw. Loose, flowing, gathered above the bust or under the bust — never across the bump itself. This is the single piece of guidance we give every family-and-maternity client.

Wind notes for the beach

Hair, hems and fabric all behave differently when there is breeze on the shore. The frame can either fight the wind or use it — and using it is the better photograph almost every time.

Hair

Loose waves and natural texture hold up far better than rigid blowouts. Braids, half-up styles, and natural curls survive the wind and look intentional in motion. A blown-out style smooths down for the first ten minutes and then turns into something the wearer cannot stop touching for the next sixty. We say this in the call sheet and we say it again here: brush, leave-in, no spray cement.

Hems and fabric

Long, flowing hems lift in the wind and become part of the composition — this is the photograph you saw in the magazine. Short, structured hems do not lift; they fight back. If you want movement, the fabric has to want it too. A maxi linen dress, a long shirt over light trousers, a kaftan that hits the ankle — these all move with the wind.

The wind direction itself

Our team checks wind forecasts the morning of every beach session. Onshore (wind blowing from sea to land) tends to push hair forward into the face — we shoot with the figure facing the wind so it sweeps away cleanly. Offshore (land to sea) pushes hair back and is the cleaner direction for portraits. Cross-shore is the photographer's problem to solve, not yours. Your job is the loose hair and the long hem.

Water-spray contingency

Salt spray is real on every beach session, especially within five metres of the surf line at high tide. We plan for it rather than fight it.

Two simple rules. First, the dressier garment goes early in the session, when we are shooting the open architecture, the dunes and the upper beach — not the wave edge. Second, anything we plan to take into the wave edge (kids playing, a couple wading, the silhouette frame at sunset) is a fabric that can dry quickly and read well wet: linen, light cotton, swimwear. Silk and structured fabrics do not go near salt water. Salt does not photograph — it shows up as a faint white residue and as creased, stiff fabric — and it ruins anything dry-clean only.

If we get caught by an unexpected swell, we pause for thirty seconds, wipe down the camera and shoot the recovery frame from the next position. The light keeps moving. We do not wait for perfect dry conditions; we work with the coast we have.

Three combinations that always work

If you have read this far and want a shortcut, here are three tested combinations for a couple or family of three to five.

  • The classic. One figure in Resort White (linen dress or shirt + trousers), one figure in Atlantic Navy (linen shirt + sand trousers), children or third figures in Warm Sand. Reads cleanest against turquoise water.
  • The warm-tonal. All figures in variations of Warm Sand — oat, camel, dune — with one figure adding Resort White as a top layer. Reads as a documentary family in editorial light. Works particularly well for sunset on the Pacific side in Los Cabos.
  • The tonal navy. Lead figure in Resort White, supporting figures in Soft Slate and a single Atlantic Navy. Holds together like a magazine cover. Works best in open shade or marina light, on the Caribbean side.

The night before

Steam every garment the evening before the session and lay them out flat. Steaming — not ironing — keeps linen soft. Pack a small mirror. Apply your last coat of sunscreen at least two hours before call time, on the matte mineral side, so the skin absorbs it cleanly instead of catching the light. Take off any jewellery you are not certain about; if you hesitate at the door, leave it.

The clients who arrive with the strongest galleries are the ones who decided their wardrobe two days before and stopped thinking about it. The light decides the photograph. The wardrobe just gets out of its way.
