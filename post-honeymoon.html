Honeymoon photography — day 2 or 3, never day 1.

The wedding is the photograph everyone planned. The honeymoon is the photograph you actually wanted to remember — just the two of you, off the clock, on the beach where you are already staying. Here is how IVAE Studios schedules it so the light, the rhythm and the resort all line up.

April 2, 2026 · ~6 min read · By Vianey Ortega

Most couples we photograph for a honeymoon book seven to ten nights at a single resort somewhere along the Riviera Maya. They arrive late on a Saturday after the wedding the night before. They have shoes off by Sunday lunchtime. By Monday they are finally a couple again, and not the centre of a 140-person event. That is the day we shoot.

The wedding photographs are already done. They were earned in front of family, planners, florists and a string quartet. The honeymoon session is something different. No timeline. No guests. No room for the kind of pose that survives a reception line. Just two people who made it through, on the beach they paid to be on, in the hour the light was made for them.

Day 2 or day 3 — never day 1

The single most common scheduling mistake is booking the session for the day of arrival. Day 1 is travel fatigue: the dehydration of the flight, the sun glare of the transfer, the half-unpacked suitcase, the wedding-day exhaustion still on the face. The body reads it on camera even when the brain doesn't.

The other mistake is leaving the session for the back half of the trip. By day 5 the rhythm of the resort has set in. You are in vacation mode — sun-tired, a little sandy, on a different sleep cycle. The photographs read as vacation, not as honeymoon.

Day 2 or day 3 is the window. Long enough to have shaken off the flight and the wedding. Soon enough that you are still wearing the trip rather than worn by it. We schedule the session for one of those two evenings, sunset only, on the beach attached to your resort.

The trip you actually wanted to remember happens on day 2 or day 3.

Sunset, at your resort beach

We do not load couples into a car for a honeymoon session. The resort beach is almost always the right answer, for three reasons that nobody tells you in advance.

First, you are already there. The session begins thirty steps from your room. There is no transfer time, no lobby check-out, no driver waiting. You arrive at your own pace and walk back to dinner the same way.

Second, the beach is the set you bought into. The architecture, the palms, the water register — all of it is the reason you booked this property and not another one a kilometre down the coast. The honeymoon photograph that sits on your wall five years from now should look like the trip, not like a generic stretch of sand.

Third, the sunset belongs to the resort. By the time golden hour arrives, the day-tripper crowds are gone, the beach attendants have stacked the loungers, and the property is quiet. We schedule the session to start about seventy minutes before sunset, with the first frames at the warmest end of the curve and the last frames in blue hour, when the sky is magenta and the water has gone ink.

What the session actually feels like

Sixty to ninety minutes. You walk in something you would wear to a quiet dinner — ivory linen, a slip dress, a pair of barefoot sandals. We do not ask for a second outfit. The session is one continuous arc rather than a wardrobe carousel.

Direction is gentle and specific. Walk toward the water. Whisper something only the other one hears. Look at the horizon, then at each other, in that order. By minute fifteen most couples have stopped checking the camera. By minute thirty the photographs are quiet and unguarded — the kind of frames that survive being looked at often.

We do not interrupt for golden-hour clichés. No champagne flutes pretending to clink. No dipping the bride backwards. The wedding album already has those. The honeymoon gallery is intentionally calmer — the version of you that finally exhaled.

What to bring

Almost nothing. Bare feet. One outfit per person, ironed the night before. Whatever jewellery is part of the new chapter (rings, an heirloom). The lipstick that lasted dinner the night before, applied late so it photographs warm rather than wet. We send a one-page brief twenty-four hours before the session with the exact start time, the meeting spot at the resort, the wind read for that evening, and a contingency in case of passing weather.

Where the resort is

Most of our honeymoon sessions happen along the Riviera Maya — the curve from Playa del Carmen through Akumal down to Tulum — because that is where most couples we work with stay. The south-southeast orientation of the coastline is unusually generous in golden hour. Sunset light bounces off the limestone shelf and gives the beach a warmer cast than the open Atlantic of Cancún or the Pacific copper of Los Cabos. Cenote sessions sit outside the honeymoon brief; they are their own discipline and we recommend them as a separate add-on rather than a substitute for the beach hour.

If your honeymoon overlaps a destination wedding we are already covering — see our luxury weddings work — the honeymoon session usually slots in two evenings after the ceremony, on the same property, with the same team. It is the quietest gallery of the trip and the one most couples reach for first when they get home.
